UX DESIGN

OVERVIEW



BACKGROUND

For my senior Capstone project, I designed a mobile web application called MediSync with a team of three of my peers. We were given the freedom to choose our idea and create a working prototype by the end of the project.

PROBLEM

Prescription drug overdose is the leading cause of injury death in the United States. Among them, 79.9% were unintentional. Taking drugs properly and at the proper times is crucial in preventing these accidents.

GOAL

Create a mobile web application that helps users schedule drugs and monitor for harmful interactions between those drugs.

Project Details

Nina Dang, David Gutierrez, Troy Griffiths, Brett Yamada
20 Weeks

RESEARCH

COMPETITOR ANALYSIS


We began by looking for existing applications that tap into the mobile medical space. Medisafe is a native mobile application that allows users to schedule drugs, paired with a social aspect that allows families and friends to share their schedules for caretaking purposes. However, there were no interaction detection features.

+: Thoroughly designed, clean interface, reliable scheduler.
-: Lack of interaction detection, skeumorphic design may introduce usability difficulties

Another popular application is WebMD. WebMD is well-known in the online health community as a reliable source for self-diagnosis, and has a feature that checks for interactions between drugs. No scheduling features are present.

+: Drug interaction checker, reliable source.
-: No scheduling functionality.


USER RESEARCH

We contacted a Pharmacy Techinician at the University of Washington Medical Center to interview. From that, we learned that a lot of the issues that arise with drug interactions come from the introduction of over the counter drugs (OTCs). OTCs don't require a pharmacists' discretion, which is when problems begin to occur and patients unknowingly take drugs that interact.

Next we created an online, anonymous survey consiting of open-ended and likert-scale questions about one's medication habits, pharmaceutical experiences, and mobile application use. We received about 80 responses. Our major insight was that the majority of our respondents (80%) do actively use online resources like WebMD to find medical information, with the rest resorting to drug labels and their pharmacists. From this, we affirmed the relevance of our idea to the market, and decidedly aimed to create an application that assists users without replacing their pharmacist.

DESIGN

STRATEGY

There are three central features to the design of MediSync. The first being the drug scheduler, which allows for users to create a personalized schedule and also alerts the user when a they attempt to input a drug that interacts harmfully with their existing medications. The second feature is an ability to input two or more drugs into a checker, providing information about interactions without the need for a schedule. The last feature allows users to search for drug information about dosage, instructions, side effects, and other common drug label categories.

AN UNFORESEEN EVENT

Not being used to developing 8 hours a day (apart from my traditional school days), the frontend implementation was finished by week 6. The original scope was to finish the design by the 12 week span of my internship. For the remaining time, I decided to dive into the backend and see the project to full completion.

BACKEND

Having zero backend experience, I researched different languages and servers that I could learn to implement the "Recently Viewed" feature. I learned PHP and used it to dynamically retrieve and display the recent dasboards for the user.

TESTING

BlizzStats was released to three employees for the first round of testing. After surveying their experiences and fixing concerns, it was released to the Business Intelligence department for more usability tests. I surveyed them after a week of use, and the results affirmed a positive experience.
